Bivariate Choropleth Map

These are my two bivariate choropleth maps. The left is median income and elevation, and the right is median monthly rental cost and elevation. The areas with low income/monthly rent and low elevation are coloured pale green/yellow. We can see the palest colour mainly in Richmond, and sprinkled in a few other places. We can see low elevation and a slightly higher rent/income in many places around Vancouver as well. This map shows that there could be a relationship between where we see low elevations, and low income/rent. This alone however, is not enough to confirm that there is a spatial relationship.

Conclusion

Taken altogether, the results I came to lead to the conclusion that there may be a relationship between elevation and vulnerability. I did not analyze enough variables to be certain of that relationship, and did not create a vulnerability index. Given the background research that is included here, I would say it is extremely likely that there is a relationship between elevation and vulnerability, specifically that individuals with a higher social vulnerability index are at a higher risk of having their lives uprooted by climate change. By not having the resources required to bounce back from a disaster, to not having stable shelter, potentially living at lower elevations, the upper class will end up on top during climate change. These issues are magnified today, in a pandemic, individuals with higher vulnerability are put at the highest risk. Homeless individuals have a much tougher time self-isolating during COVID-19 than individuals who can afford shelter.
